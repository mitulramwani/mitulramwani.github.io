# From IC to Manager: What Nobody Tells You

The hardest part of becoming a manager isn't learning new skills — it's unlearning the ones that made you a great engineer.

When I made the transition, I thought the job was about being the best technical person in the room. I was wrong. The job is about making everyone else in the room better.

## The Identity Shift

As an individual contributor, your value is visible. You write code, ship features, fix bugs. You can point to a diff and say "I did that." The feedback loop is tight and satisfying.

As a manager, your output is invisible. Your best work happens in a 1:1 where you help someone see a problem differently. It shows up in a team that ships without drama, in an engineer who gets promoted, in a culture where people feel safe raising concerns.

This invisibility is disorienting at first. You have to learn to find satisfaction in outcomes you didn't directly produce.

## The Hardest Unlearning: Jumping to Solutions

Engineers are trained to solve problems. See a problem → fix it. This instinct is valuable as an IC and actively harmful as a manager.

When an engineer comes to you with a problem, your first instinct will be to solve it for them. Resist this. Every time you solve their problem, you're robbing them of a growth opportunity and signaling that you don't trust them.

Instead, ask questions. "What have you tried?" "What do you think the options are?" "What would you do if I weren't here?" Your job is to help them develop their own judgment, not to demonstrate yours.

## The Loneliness Nobody Mentions

There's a loneliness to management that nobody warns you about. You're no longer fully part of the team in the way you were as an IC. There are things you know that you can't share — performance issues, org changes, difficult feedback about someone's peers. You carry context that creates distance.

The antidote is peer relationships with other managers. Find people at your level — inside or outside your company — with whom you can be candid. This is one of the most important investments you can make early in your management career.

## What Actually Matters in Year One

If I were advising someone making the transition today, I'd say: spend your first year on three things.

**Build trust before you build anything else.** Your team needs to believe you're in their corner before they'll take risks, raise problems, or be honest with you. This takes time and consistency. Show up, follow through, and always have their back in rooms they're not in.

**Learn to give feedback early and often.** Most new managers wait too long to give difficult feedback, hoping things will improve on their own. They rarely do. Kind feedback delivered early is almost always better than honest feedback delivered late.

**Resist the urge to prove yourself technically.** You'll be tempted to dive into the code, to be the one who finds the bug, to demonstrate that you still have it. This is ego. Your team doesn't need you to be the best engineer — they need you to be the best manager.

## The Reward

Here's what nobody tells you about the upside: there is nothing quite like watching someone you've invested in grow into their potential. When an engineer you hired gets promoted, when a team you built ships something remarkable, when someone you mentored becomes a manager themselves — that's a different kind of satisfaction than shipping code. It's slower to arrive, but it runs deeper.

The transition from IC to manager is one of the hardest career moves in engineering. But for those who make it well, it's also one of the most rewarding.
