# The Amazon SDM Interview: What I Wish I'd Known

The Amazon SDM (Software Development Manager) interview loop is one of the most rigorous hiring processes in the industry. Having been through it and having interviewed many SDM candidates since, here's what I wish someone had told me going in.

## What Amazon Is Actually Looking For

Amazon hires SDMs to own outcomes, not manage processes. The distinction matters enormously for how you prepare.

An SDM at Amazon is expected to have deep enough technical judgment to make architectural decisions, challenge their team's proposals, and earn the respect of strong engineers — while simultaneously being able to hire, develop, and retain talent, drive operational excellence, and deliver against business goals.

The bar is high because the role is genuinely hard. You're being evaluated on whether you can do all of these things at once, under ambiguity, at Amazon's pace.

## The Loop Structure

A typical SDM loop includes:

**Screening call** with a recruiter (30 min) — mostly logistics, background, and a few behavioral questions. Be concise and direct.

**Hiring manager interview** (60 min) — deep dive on your background, leadership style, and how you've handled specific situations. This sets the tone for the loop.

**Technical interview** (60 min) — system design, technical judgment, and your ability to engage with complex engineering problems without necessarily writing code.

**Leadership and behavioral interviews** (2–3 x 60 min) — structured around Amazon's Leadership Principles. Every interviewer will probe specific LPs in depth.

**Bar raiser interview** (60 min) — conducted by an independent interviewer from another team. They're specifically looking for reasons to slow down the hire. Be your most thoughtful self here.

## Leadership Principles: Go Deeper Than You Think

Every Amazon interview involves the Leadership Principles. Most candidates prepare surface-level answers. The bar raiser will dig several layers deeper.

Don't just prepare a story for each LP — prepare to be interrogated about it. "Tell me about a time you had to make a decision with incomplete data" will be followed by "What data did you have? What did you decide not to wait for? What would have changed your decision? What happened afterward? What would you do differently?"

Your stories need to be genuinely yours — specific, detailed, and honest about what went wrong. Polished stories that sound like case studies are a yellow flag. Stories where you clearly learned something hard are a green flag.

The LPs I'd pay particular attention to for SDM: **Hire and Develop the Best**, **Have Backbone; Disagree and Commit**, **Deliver Results**, **Dive Deep**, and **Are Right, A Lot**.

## The Technical Interview: What It's Really Testing

The SDM technical interview is not a coding interview. You won't be asked to implement algorithms. You will be asked to design systems, make technical tradeoffs, and demonstrate that you can engage credibly with senior engineers on hard problems.

Prepare system design questions — distributed systems, scalability, reliability. But more importantly, practice explaining your reasoning out loud. Interviewers want to see how you think, not just what conclusion you reach.

They're also looking for intellectual honesty. "I'm not sure about the best approach here — I'd want to consult with my team's infrastructure experts" is a much better answer than confidently stating something incorrect.

## The Bar Raiser: Don't Treat It Differently

Many candidates try to detect who the bar raiser is and perform differently in that interview. This is a mistake for two reasons: you'll often guess wrong, and inconsistency across your loop is itself a signal.

The bar raiser's job is to evaluate whether you raise the bar for Amazon — whether hiring you makes Amazon better than not hiring you. They're looking for genuine strengths, not the absence of weaknesses.

Be yourself. Be consistent. Be honest about what you don't know.

## The Most Common Mistake

The most common mistake I see SDM candidates make is underselling the scale and complexity of what they've done.

Amazon interviewers are calibrated to Amazon's scale. If you led a team that built a system processing millions of requests per day, say that — don't just say "a high-traffic system." If you managed a team of 12 engineers across two locations, say that. Specificity signals credibility.

At the same time, don't overclaim. If your team was 6 people and you say 15, you'll be caught. Accuracy matters more than impressiveness.

## Preparing for the Loop

Six to eight weeks of focused preparation is reasonable for most candidates. I'd structure it roughly as:

- **Weeks 1–2:** Audit your career stories against every LP. Find gaps. Live experiences you don't have good stories for.
- **Weeks 3–4:** System design practice. Pick two or three domains relevant to your experience and go deep.
- **Weeks 5–6:** Mock interviews. Find a peer who can ask hard follow-up questions and give you honest feedback.
- **Week 7–8:** Review and refine. Tighten your stories. Revisit weak areas.

## Final Thought

The Amazon interview is hard, and it should be. The role is hard. If you're well-prepared, honest, and genuinely reflect on your experiences rather than packaging them, you'll give yourself the best possible chance.

Good luck.
